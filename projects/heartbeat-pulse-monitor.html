📖 Introduction

A photoplethysmography (PPG) sensor like the KY-039 shines an infrared LED through your fingertip while a phototransistor on the opposite side measures how much light passes through. With each heartbeat, the blood vessels in your fingertip expand slightly, scattering more IR light and causing a tiny dip in the sensor's output value — this is the pulse signal your Arduino needs to detect and time.

The signal is very small and noisy, so processing it requires an important algorithm trio: a running average to filter DC drift, a peak-detection routine to identify each heartbeat, and a rolling average of inter-beat intervals to compute stable BPM. Mastering these three techniques unlocks a huge category of signal processing projects — from ECG simulation to step counters and vibration monitors.

⚙️ How It Works

  1. 1

    Sample the Sensor Signal

    Connect the KY-039 signal pin to A0. In a tight loop, call analogRead(A0) every 10 ms (100 Hz) and store the last 20 readings in a circular buffer. This gives you a smooth waveform to analyse.

  2. 2

    Apply a Running Average Filter

    Subtract the running average from each new reading to remove slow DC drift from ambient light and finger position changes. The resulting signal oscillates around zero with peaks on each heartbeat.

  3. 3

    Detect Peaks and Compute BPM

    When the filtered value crosses a positive threshold, record the timestamp with millis() and flash the LED. Store the last 5 inter-beat intervals in an array. Average them and compute: BPM = 60000 / avgInterval.

  4. 4

    Display and Validate

    Print the BPM to the LCD. Add a validity check: only display if BPM is between 40 and 200, otherwise show "Place Finger". Update the display every 2 seconds to avoid rapid flickering between readings.
