📖 Introduction

A thermostat is a closed-loop control system: it measures an output (temperature) and adjusts an input (fan speed) to try to keep things in balance. This is the simplest form of feedback control — the foundation of PID controllers used in drones, 3D printers, ovens, and industrial processes.

The DS18B20 is a 1-Wire digital temperature sensor ±0.5°C accurate from −55°C to +125°C. A single data wire (with a 4.7 kΩ pull-up) communicates a full 12-bit temperature reading — no ADC noise, no calibration drift. The fan is driven via an N-MOSFET transistor (e.g. 2N7000 or IRLZ44N) to safely switch motor current from the Arduino's 5 mA logic pin.

⚙️ How It Works

  1. 1
    Wire the DS18B20 — Red (VCC) → 5 V, Black (GND) → GND, Yellow (Data) → D4 with 4.7 kΩ pull-up to 5 V. MOSFET: Gate → D9 (~), Drain → Fan –, Source → GND. Fan + → 5 V. Place flyback diode across fan terminals (banded end to +).
  2. 2
    Read temperature — Include OneWire and DallasTemperature libraries. sensors.requestTemperatures(); float tempC = sensors.getTempCByIndex(0);. Reading takes ~750 ms for 12-bit resolution — use setWaitForConversion(false) and millis() timing to avoid blocking.
  3. 3
    Proportional speed ramp — Define a comfort range: below 28°C = fan off (PWM 0). 28–40°C = map linearly: int speed = map(tempC, 28, 40, 60, 255); — 60 is the minimum PWM to keep the motor spinning (stall threshold). Above 40°C = full speed 255.
  4. 4
    Display and hysteresis — Print temperature and fan % to LCD. Add hysteresis of ±1°C: once the fan turns off, don't re-enable until temp exceeds 29°C (not just 28°C). This prevents rapid on/off cycling — a key real-world thermostat technique.
